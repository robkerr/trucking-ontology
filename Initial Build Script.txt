So — what is an ontology? The word comes from philosophy, where it literally means 'the study of what exists.' But in data and analytics, it means something much more practical. 

An ontology is a structured representation of the things in your business, the properties those things have, and the relationships between them. Think of it as a map of your business domain — not the data itself, but the meaning behind the data. 

Now here's where it gets interesting. A well-designed ontology in a data analysis context brings together three layers: 

First — Data. This is the information you use to make a decision. Your tables, your events, your telemetry. The raw material. 

Second — Logic. This is the reasoning you apply to evaluate a decision. Business rules, constraints, thresholds — the 'if this, then that' of your domain. 

Third — Action. This is the execution of the decision. What happens when the logic says 'go.' An alert fires, a workflow kicks off, an agent takes a step. 

Most analytics platforms stop at Data. Maybe they get you partway into Logic with some calculated measures or KPIs. But an ontology connects all three — and that's what makes it powerful. It's not just a semantic layer. It's a decision layer. 

 

Now, ontologies aren't a new idea. Knowledge graphs have been around for years. But what Microsoft is doing with the Ontology item in Fabric is making this accessible inside the platform you're already using for analytics. 

The ontology in Fabric is part of the IQ workload. You define entity types — like a Customer, an Order, a Truck, a Sensor — and you give them properties and relationships. Then you bind those definitions to your actual data in OneLake. Lakehouse tables, Eventhouse streams, semantic models — it all connects. 

What you get is a queryable graph where relationships are first-class citizens. They're not buried in join logic or foreign key lookups. They're explicit, governed, and navigable. And because it's Fabric, queries get pushed down to the native engines — GQL for the graph, KQL for the event data — so it performs at scale. 

And here's the kicker — the ontology also supports natural language queries. You can ask business questions in plain English, and the Natural Language 2 `Ontology layer translates that into structured queries against your ontology. That's a big deal for business users and AI agents alike. 

 

Alright, let's talk about design. Because the number one mistake people make with ontologies is jumping straight into the tool without thinking through the model first. 

Designing an ontology comes down to three questions. What are the things in my domain? What properties do those things have? And how do those things relate to each other? 

Let's say we're modeling a trucking company. Our entities might be Truck, Driver, Trip, and Location. Each of those has properties — a Truck has a VIN, a capacity, a status. A Trip has an origin, a destination, a departure time. 

Now — relationships. And this is where good design really matters. The best practice from ontology and graph modeling is to name your relationships as active verbs that read naturally as a sentence. 

So instead of some vague link between Truck and Trip, you define: Truck performs Trip. That reads like a sentence. It has a clear direction. And it follows the natural 'many-to-few' pattern — one truck performs many trips over time. 

You can optionally define the inverse — Trip performed-by Truck. But the primary relationship should always be that active, verb-based edge. Avoid passive phrasing like 'made-by' or 'assigned-to' as your primary direction — those obscure the semantics and make traversal harder. 

In practice with Fabric's ontology, use the active relationship as your canonical edge, and let the graph traversal handle reverse queries. Don't duplicate semantics unless you have a specific reason for it — like a policy rule or an inference pattern.
